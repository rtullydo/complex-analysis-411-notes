We're setting up to invoke a sort of dominated integral theorem from Newman's original paper, so we now introduce functions \(f\) and \(g\text{.}\) Define the functions

\begin{align*} f(t) \amp= \th(e^t)e^{-t} - 1\\ g(s) \amp= \frac{\Phi(s+1)}{s+1} - \frac{1}{s} \end{align*}

By Step III, \(\th\) is \(O(x)\text{,}\) and so \(f\) is bounded. Proceeding formally for a moment, (that is, ignoring issues of convergence, which will be pinned down shortly) the same change of variables from above will give

\begin{equation*} \int_1^\infty \frac{\th(x) - x}{x^2} \, dx = \int_0^\infty f(t) \, dt. \end{equation*}
